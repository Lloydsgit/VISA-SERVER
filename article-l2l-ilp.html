Ledger to Ledger Transfers and the Interledger Protocol: A Plain-English Guide

Summary

"Ledger to Ledger" sounds intimidating, but the idea is simple. Every bank, payment app, and even every blockchain keeps a list of who owns what. When value moves between two of those lists, that's an L2L transfer.

The mechanics behind it — correspondent banks, message formats, nostro accounts, interoperability protocols — are where things get dense. This guide unpacks them in plain English, with a glossary at the end so you can scan back when an acronym blurs together.

What L2L Actually Means

L2L is short for Ledger-to-Ledger. It describes moving value between two separate ledgers, and there are two very different flavors depending on whether you're in traditional banking or in blockchain.

In Traditional Banking

A ledger is just a record-keeping system. A bank's ledger tracks every account, every balance, every credit and every debit. When money moves from your account to someone else's, no physical cash is teleported anywhere. The bank simply adjusts entries — your balance goes down, theirs goes up.

If both accounts are at the same bank, that's an intra-bank L2L. If the two ledgers belong to different banks, that's an inter-bank L2L — and it requires the global banking system to coordinate.

In Blockchain

The same idea applies, but the ledgers are distributed. Moving value from one blockchain to another requires specialized tools: atomic swaps, cross-chain bridges, or interoperability protocols. The principle is identical — one ledger decrements, another increments — but the trust model and tooling differ.

Mental model. A ledger is a spreadsheet of who owns what. An L2L transfer is a coordinated edit to two of those spreadsheets at once.

The Three Layers Behind Every Bank

Before going further, it helps to picture the inside of a modern bank. Banks don't run on one big system — they run on three deliberately separated layers, each with its own job and its own security posture.

1. The Middle Layer (API and Integration Hub)

This is the bank's digital front door. It's where mobile apps, internet banking, partners, and external networks plug in. The middle layer authenticates requests, translates between data formats, enforces rate limits, and routes incoming traffic. It's flexible, modern, and frequently updated — but it doesn't move money on its own.

2. The Core Banking System

This is the operational engine. It holds customer accounts, applies product rules like interest and fees, processes transactions, and updates balances in real time. Common platforms include Temenos T24, Finastra Fusion, Oracle FLEXCUBE, FIS Profile, and Mambu. The core is more stable and more guarded than the middle layer, and importantly, it isn't directly reachable from the outside world.

3. The Ledger Server

This is the most isolated layer. The ledger server keeps the authoritative double-entry record of every financial movement at the institution — debits balance credits, always. It generates regulatory reports, supports the chart of accounts, and is often deployed in a physically separate data center, behind extra firewalls, with multi-factor authentication and hardware security modules. It does not talk to the internet.

Why three layers? Defense in depth. Even if an attacker breaks the middle layer, they still face the core. Even if they break the core, they still have to cross the ledger server's wall. Each layer also evolves at its own pace — the middle layer can ship weekly while the ledger server changes once a quarter.

Why Funds Can't Be Downloaded

A common misconception — often spread by scams — is that you can somehow "download" funds from a bank's ledger or core server. You can't. Here's why:

  • The ledger and core systems are not internet-facing. They sit behind multiple network boundaries and only accept requests through narrowly defined internal interfaces.
  • Funds aren't files. There is no folder called /balances/ sitting on a hard drive that anyone could copy. A balance is an entry in a tightly controlled database, protected by encryption at rest, integrity checks, and audit logging.
  • Double-entry accounting prevents one-sided moves. Every change to that database has to be paired with a corresponding entry elsewhere. You can't increase your balance without simultaneously decreasing someone else's — and that someone else has to actually agree.
  • Real fund movement always passes through the regulated framework of the global banking system. The integrity of every transaction depends on that, and so does compliance with anti-money-laundering and sanctions rules.

Instructions vs. Actual Settlement

This is the single most important concept in the whole article. Banking has two distinct things that beginners often confuse:

  • A payment instruction — a message saying "please move X amount from account A to account B."
  • Settlement — the actual change in cash balances between the institutions involved.

A SWIFT MT103 message, for example, is an instruction. So is an MT202. They tell the receiving institution what should happen. Settlement is the separate step where the institutions actually move cash between their accounts to make the instruction real. The instruction message travels quickly. Settlement may take hours, the same business day, or longer depending on the rail used.

Why this matters. An instruction without settlement is just a message. A bank will not credit a customer's account based on a message alone — it will hold or pend the funds until settlement is confirmed.

Correspondent vs. Intermediary Banks

When two banks don't have a direct relationship — which is most banks, most of the time — they rely on the correspondent banking network.

Correspondent Bank

A bank that holds an account on behalf of another bank, allowing them to settle in a currency or region they don't directly operate in. This is called a nostro account from the sending bank's view and a vostro account from the holding bank's view.

Intermediary Bank

When no direct correspondent relationship exists, the payment may need to pass through one or more intermediary banks, each of which charges a fee and adds processing time.

Term Role From whose perspective
Nostro "Our account, held with another bank" Sending bank
Vostro "Your account, held with us" Holding bank
Intermediary Routing-only relay between two unconnected banks Either side

How a Bank Confirms Funds Actually Arrived

Receiving an instruction message is not the same as receiving the money. A receiving bank confirms funds have actually arrived by checking its nostro account at the relevant correspondent or central bank. Only when that balance reflects the inbound credit will it post the amount to the beneficiary's account.

For high-value or urgent payments, banks use real-time gross settlement systems where each transaction settles individually and immediately. For lower-value or bulk transactions, they may use net settlement, where many transactions are batched and the net difference is settled at intervals.

What the Interledger Protocol Is

The Interledger Protocol, or ILP, is an open standard for sending payments across different ledgers — bank ledgers, payment system ledgers, blockchain ledgers, mobile wallet ledgers — without requiring any of them to trust each other directly.

How It Works

ILP breaks a payment into many small packets, each routed through a chain of connectors. Each connector takes value on one ledger and releases equivalent value on the next, using cryptographic conditions to ensure that no participant can run off with money mid-transfer.

Why It Matters

ILP is designed to do for value what the internet protocol did for information — provide a universal routing layer that doesn't care what the underlying networks are. It's still emerging in production use, but it points to a future where moving value across systems is as routine as sending an email.

Which Rail Should You Use?

The right rail depends on three questions: how fast do you need it, what currency and geography are involved, and what level of certainty do you need on arrival? Cross-border high-value payments still default to SWIFT-based rails because of regulatory familiarity and reach. Domestic instant payments favor real-time rails like UPI, FedNow, or SEPA Instant. Digital-native operators increasingly use stablecoin-based settlement for cross-border speed and lower cost.

Rule of thumb. Speed, cost, and trust are a three-way tradeoff. Pick the two that matter most for your use case, and accept that the third will be a compromise.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is L2L just another word for a wire transfer?

Not exactly. A wire transfer is one example of an L2L flow, but L2L is the broader concept. It includes wire transfers, instant payments, blockchain transfers, and any other movement of value between two ledger systems.

Can I move money instantly between any two banks in the world?

Not today, not reliably. Domestic instant rails are widespread, but cross-border instant transfers still depend on the correspondent network or newer rails like stablecoins. Universal instant cross-border is what protocols like ILP aim to enable.

What's the safest way to send a large international transfer?

For most institutional needs, a SWIFT-based cross-border transfer with GPI tracking remains the standard. It's slower than instant rails but offers regulatory familiarity, end-to-end traceability, and broad bank coverage.

Why does it sometimes take days to receive an international payment?

Each intermediary bank in the chain may add delay for processing, compliance screening, and cut-off times. Payments crossing weekends, holidays, or multiple time zones can stack additional delays. Tracking services like SWIFT GPI now make these delays visible in near real time.
