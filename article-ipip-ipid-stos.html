Understanding IPIP, IPID, and StoS Transfers

Summary

IPIP, IPID, and StoS are three acronyms that float around in conversations about payment processing. Some are real terms used by specific institutions or systems. Some are informal labels for concepts that have proper names elsewhere. And some are jargon repurposed by less-than-reputable platforms to sound technical.

This guide goes through each one carefully, explains what people actually mean when they say it, points to the legitimate underlying concept, and shows you how to verify any banking acronym you come across.

Why These Acronyms Are Confusing

Banking and payments produce hundreds of acronyms — ACH, RTGS, SEPA, SWIFT, IBAN, BIC, EFT, NEFT, IMPS, the list goes on. Most are governed by clear standards bodies (ISO, EMVCo, national central banks) and have public documentation.

Some terms, however, are internal labels used by specific banks or systems and have never been standardized. Others are descriptive shorthand that beginners pick up from forums and then quote as if they were formal standards. And a few are deliberately invented to sound impressive in marketing material for platforms that can't cite a real standard.

The litmus test. If an acronym is real, you can find it documented at ISO, SWIFT, a central bank, or a major card network's developer site. If it only shows up on forums, marketing sites, and broker offers, treat it as suspect until proven otherwise.

IPIP — Interbank Payment Instruction Processing

IPIP is sometimes used as shorthand for the general concept of moving a payment instruction from one bank's processing system to another. It is not a formal standard. There's no ISO specification for "IPIP."

The legitimate processes that the term tries to describe are well-defined and have proper names depending on the rail and region:

  • SWIFT FIN+ — the messaging transport for ISO 20022 cross-border bank-to-bank instructions.
  • RTGS systems — Real-Time Gross Settlement systems like Fedwire (USA), CHAPS (UK), TARGET2 (Eurozone), CHAPS-equivalents in India (RTGS), Australia (RITS).
  • ACH and NEFT — net settlement rails for batched lower-value transfers.

If someone offers you "IPIP transfers" without being able to map that to one of the real systems above, ask which actual rail is being used and which licensed bank is on each end.

IPID — Internal Payment Identification

IPID is the kind of label that some banks use internally for a payment's identifier within their own processing system. It's analogous to a transaction reference number, but the term is not part of any external standard.

The real equivalents that exist in published standards:

  • UETR — Unique End-to-End Transaction Reference, the 36-character identifier used by SWIFT GPI and now embedded in ISO 20022 messages.
  • STAN — System Trace Audit Number, the short identifier used in ISO 8583 card messages.
  • RRN — Retrieval Reference Number, the longer card-transaction identifier.
  • End-to-end ID — a structured ID field in ISO 20022 pacs.008 messages.

If you receive a payment notification and someone refers to its IPID, they likely mean "the internal reference our system uses for this payment." That's a normal thing for a bank to have. It's not a transferable instrument or a financial product on its own.

Store-to-Store (StoS)

"Store-to-Store" is descriptive language sometimes used to describe value transfer between two retail or commercial outlets. In legitimate use, the concept maps to a few different real mechanisms:

  • Closed-loop network transfers — like moving balance between two locations of the same retailer's gift card system.
  • Visa Direct or Mastercard Send — the network-level push payment products used to credit funds to a card. Real, well-documented, requires licensed acquirer participation.
  • Money transfer operators — companies like Western Union, MoneyGram, Wise, Remitly, Ria. They move value between physical and digital "stores" of value globally and are licensed money transmitters.
  • Internal CBS transfers — within a single bank's core banking system, moving balance between two of its branches (which are sometimes called stores in internal language).
Note. "Store-to-Store transfer" on its own is not a standard. The legitimate mechanism behind it always has a more specific name. Ask which one.

How They Relate to Each Other

The clearest mental model: IPIP is about how a payment instruction moves, IPID is what identifies that instruction, and StoS is one of the possible end-to-end value transfer patterns. None of them are formal categories in the same sense that ISO 8583 message types or ISO 20022 message families are.

When you encounter these terms in a conversation:

  1. Ask which actual rail or network the underlying transfer uses.
  2. Ask which licensed institutions are on the sender and receiver side.
  3. Ask for the official message format and reference identifier.

If the answers don't map to anything documented at a standards body or central bank, you're dealing with informal language at best and marketing fluff at worst.

How to Verify Any Banking Term

A practical checklist anyone can run in two minutes:

  1. Search the term at iso.org and iso20022.org. Real standards are documented there.
  2. Search at the relevant card network's developer site. developer.visa.com, developer.mastercard.com, developer.americanexpress.com.
  3. Search at SWIFT (swift.com) and the central bank of the country involved. RBI, Federal Reserve, ECB, BoE, MAS.
  4. Search for a public Postman collection or OpenAPI spec. Real APIs publish them.
  5. Search at the national payments council. NPCI in India, NACHA in the US, EPC in Europe.
  6. If a term shows up only on forums, broker websites, and offer emails — and nowhere in the official sources above — treat it as informal or fabricated until proven otherwise.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is IPIP a real banking standard?

Not in any published international standard. Some banks may use it internally as a label. If someone is offering "IPIP transfers" commercially, ask which actual rail (SWIFT, RTGS, ACH, NEFT, etc.) and which licensed banks are involved.

Can I send money "Store-to-Store" between banks?

You can send money between banks. The mechanism is one of the well-defined rails — SWIFT for cross-border, RTGS or ACH-equivalents for domestic, Visa Direct or Mastercard Send for card-rail push payments. "Store-to-Store" isn't itself a transfer type.

Should I trust a platform that uses these terms?

Trust comes from licenses, not vocabulary. A legitimate platform will be able to tell you its regulator (RBI, FCA, FinCEN MSB, SEC, etc.), its license number, its sponsor banks, and its actual rail. If a platform only uses informal acronyms and can't produce that information, treat it with extreme caution.

Where do these informal terms come from?

Mostly from internal documentation that leaked into informal conversation, from forum discussions where people invented shorthand, and from marketing material designed to sound technical. Some are also borrowed from real but obscure internal banking labels and then misapplied externally.
